About Our Charity

In rural areas, period poverty is a significant issue exacerbated by economic, educational, and infrastructural challenges. Many individuals in these regions face financial constraints that make purchasing menstrual products such as pads, tampons, or menstrual cups unaffordable. This often forces them to resort to using unhygienic alternatives like rags, leaves, or even sand, leading to serious health risks such as infections and reproductive health problems. The lack of comprehensive menstrual health education further perpetuates misinformation and stigma surrounding menstruation, making it difficult for girls and women to manage their periods effectively. Schools in rural areas frequently lack proper sanitation facilities, including clean water, private restrooms, and disposal systems for menstrual waste, causing many girls to miss school during their periods. This absenteeism results in significant educational setbacks and contributes to higher dropout rates. Addressing period poverty in rural areas requires a multifaceted approach, including providing affordable menstrual products, improving sanitation infrastructure, offering education on menstrual health, and challenging cultural stigmas.

Menstruation Cycle

The menstrual cycle prepares your body for pregnancy. If you are not pregnant, your hormones send a signal to your uterus to shed its lining. This becomes your period. Once you start your period, the cycle starts again. Understanding your menstrual cycle can help you track your health.
